Minimum Wage Rates (2025)

Minimum wage is the lowest wage that employers can legally pay their workers. It varies by region and is set by the Regional Tripartite Wages and Productivity Boards (RTWPB). Why does the Philippines not have the same minimum wage?

The Philippines does not have a single national minimum wage because the economic conditions, cost of living, and business capacities vary greatly from one region to another. This decentralized approach aims to create a fair wage system that is responsive to local realities.

1. Differences in the Cost of Living

The main reason for having different minimum wages is the variation in the cost of living across regions. Expenses for housing, food, transportation, and utilities are significantly higher in urban centers like Metro Manila compared to rural provinces.

2. Regional Economic Differences

The economic strength and industrial development of each region also influence wage levels. Highly industrialized regions with more businesses and investments can generally support higher wages than agricultural regions with fewer economic opportunities.

3. Regional Wage Boards and the Law

Under the Republic Act No. 6727, also known as the Wage Rationalization Act of 1989, the government established Regional Tripartite Wages and Productivity Boards (RTWPBs) in every region to determine and set minimum wage rates locally.

4. Balancing Workers’ Welfare and Business Sustainability

Another important reason for regional wage differences is to balance the welfare of workers with the sustainability of businesses. A wage that is too high for a particular region could force small businesses to close, leading to job losses.

5. Promoting Regional Growth and Competitiveness

Having different minimum wages can also encourage regional competitiveness. It may incentivize companies to invest in regions with lower operational costs, helping to distribute economic growth more evenly across the country.

What causes the increase of Minimum Wage?

The increase in minimum wage in the Philippines is caused by several economic, social, and political factors. Wage increases are not automatic — they are carefully studied and approved by the Regional Tripartite Wages and Productivity Boards (RTWPBs) based on the needs of workers and the ability of employers to pay.

Challenges and Issues in the Minimum Wage System

Despite regular adjustments, the system faces many challenges that affect both workers and employers, making it difficult to balance decent wages with economic stability.

  • 1.Mismatch with Cost of Living: Often, wage increases do not keep up with the actual cost of living.
  • 2.Regional Wage Inequality: Large gaps between regions can lead to internal migration and strain urban resources.
  • 3.Impact on SMEs: Small and Medium Enterprises struggle to afford wage hikes, sometimes leading to closures.
  • 4.Non-Compliance: Many businesses in the informal sector do not adhere to minimum wage laws.
  • 5.Inflationary Effects: Wage hikes can sometimes lead to price increases, eroding the gains for workers.
  • 6.Political Influence: Wage decisions can be influenced by political pressures rather than purely economic data.

Areas for Reform and Proposals

Various reforms have been suggested to make the system more effective and fair for both employees and employers.

  • 1.National Minimum Wage: A proposal to establish a single, nationwide minimum wage to reduce inequality.
  • 2.Automatic Adjustments: Tying wage increases automatically to inflation to ensure timeliness.
  • 3.Linking to Productivity: Basing wage hikes on productivity gains to ensure sustainability for businesses.
  • 4.Improved Enforcement: Stricter monitoring and penalties for non-compliant employers.
  • 5.Support for SMEs: Providing subsidies or tax breaks to help small businesses afford higher wages.
  • 6.Updating Living Wage Standards: Regularly reassessing the real "Family Living Wage" as a basis for adjustments.

Key Monetary Benefits

Beyond the basic wage, the Labor Code of the Philippines mandates several other monetary benefits for employees. Here are some of the most important ones, with formulas and examples.

Overtime & Premium Pay (p. 10)

Employees are entitled to additional compensation for work rendered beyond the normal eight (8) hours a day. This includes Overtime Pay for excess hours on a regular workday and Premium Pay for work performed on non-working days.

Formula (Regular OT): (Hourly Rate × 125%) × OT Hours

Ex (NCR @ ₱695/day): For 2 OT hours: (₱86.88 × 1.25) × 2 = ₱217.20

Ex (Pampanga @ ₱570/day): For 2 OT hours: (₱71.25 × 1.25) × 2 = ₱178.13

Holiday Pay (p. 15)

If an employee works on a regular holiday, they receive 200% of their wage. If they don't work, they still receive 100% of their wage, provided they were not absent without pay on the preceding workday.

Formula (Work on Holiday): Daily Rate × 200%

Ex (NCR @ ₱695/day): Pay is ₱695 × 2 = ₱1,390.00

Ex (Pampanga @ ₱570/day): Pay is ₱570 × 2 = ₱1,140.00

Night Shift Differential (p. 13)

An additional 10% on top of the regular wage for every hour of work performed between 10:00 PM and 6:00 AM. This is paid on top of any applicable overtime or holiday premium.

Formula (Night Diff): (Hourly Rate × 10%) × Night Hours

Ex (NCR @ ₱695/day): For 8 night hours: (₱86.88 × 0.10) × 8 = ₱69.44

Ex (Pampanga @ ₱570/day): For 8 night hours: (₱71.25 × 0.10) × 8 = ₱57.00

13th Month Pay (p. 25)

A mandatory benefit for all rank-and-file employees who have worked for at least one month. It must be paid on or before December 24th. It excludes allowances and other monetary benefits not part of the basic salary.

13th Month Pay Formula:

(Total Basic Salary in Year) / 12

Ex: If total annual basic pay is ₱300,000, the 13th month pay is ₱25,000.00

Separation Pay (p. 30)

Financial assistance for employees separated due to authorized causes like redundancy, retrenchment to prevent losses, or closure of an establishment.

Formula (Redundancy):

Monthly Salary × Years of Service

Ex: A worker with 5 years service at ₱25k/mo gets ₱25,000 × 5 = ₱125,000.00

Retirement Pay (p. 35)

Upon retirement age (60-65), an employee with at least five years of service is entitled to this pay. The "one-half month salary" is legally defined as ~22.5 days per year of service.

Retirement Pay Formula:

(Daily Rate × 22.5) × Years of Service

Ex (NCR @ ₱695/day): For 10 years: (₱695 × 22.5) × 10 = ₱156,375.00

Ex (Pampanga @ ₱570/day): For 10 years: (₱570 × 22.5) × 10 = ₱128,250.00

Statutory Leave Benefits

Employees are entitled to various types of leave, ensuring they have time for rest, family, and health needs.

Service Incentive Leave (SIL)

Every employee who has rendered at least one year of service is entitled to 5 days of leave with pay. This can be used for vacation or sick leave. Unused SIL at the end of the year must be converted to cash. (Handbook, p. 18)

Maternity Leave (RA 11210)

Provides 105 days of paid leave for female SSS members for live childbirth, regardless of civil status. This can be extended by 30 days without pay. Solo mothers get an additional 15 days. In case of miscarriage or emergency termination, the benefit is 60 days of paid leave. (Handbook, p. 20)

Paternity Leave (RA 8187)

Grants 7 days of paid leave to married male employees for the first four (4) deliveries of his legitimate spouse. The purpose is to allow the husband to assist his wife during her recovery and in caring for the newborn. (Handbook, p. 22)

Parental Leave for Solo Parents (RA 8972)

Provides 7 additional work days of leave with pay every year for any solo parent who has rendered service of at least one year. This leave is for performing parental duties and responsibilities where physical presence is required. (Handbook, p. 23)

Leave for VAWC Victims (RA 9262)

Female employees who are victims of violence as defined under the Anti-Violence Against Women and Their Children Act are entitled to 10 days of paid leave. This is used for attending to medical and legal concerns. (Handbook, p. 24)

SSS Benefits Overview

The Social Security System (SSS) provides a range of social insurance benefits to its members. These are funded by member and employer contributions and serve as a safety net for various life contingencies.

Sickness Benefit

A daily cash allowance for the number of days a member is unable to work due to sickness or injury.

Eligibility Summary:

  • At least 3 monthly contributions in the last 12 months.
  • Confined for at least 4 days.
  • Used up all company sick leaves.

Maternity Benefit

A daily cash allowance for female members unable to work due to childbirth, miscarriage, or emergency termination of pregnancy.

Eligibility Summary:

  • At least 3 monthly contributions in the last 12 months.
  • Provided notification to the employer.

Disability Benefit

A cash benefit, either as a monthly pension or a lump sum, for members who become permanently disabled (partially or totally).

Eligibility Summary:

  • Monthly Pension: At least 36 monthly contributions.
  • Lump Sum: Less than 36 monthly contributions.

Retirement Benefit

A cash benefit, either as a monthly pension or a lump sum, for members who can no longer work due to old age.

Eligibility Summary:

  • At least 60 (optional) or 65 (technical) years old.
  • At least 120 monthly contributions.

Death Benefit

A cash benefit, either as a monthly pension or lump sum, given to the beneficiaries of a deceased member.

Eligibility Summary:

  • Monthly Pension: Deceased paid at least 36 monthly contributions.
  • Lump Sum: Deceased paid less than 36 monthly contributions.

Funeral Benefit

A variable cash amount given to the person who paid for the burial expenses of a deceased member.

Eligibility Summary:

  • The deceased member must have been a covered SSS member.
  • Claimant must provide proof of payment for funeral expenses.

Unemployment Insurance

A one-time cash benefit for members who are involuntarily separated from employment (e.g., redundancy, retrenchment).

Eligibility Summary:

  • Not over 60 years of age at time of separation.
  • At least 36 monthly contributions, with 12 in the last 18 months.
  • Has not claimed this benefit in the last 3 years.

Understanding the SSS MPF Program (WISP)

The Mandatory Provident Fund (MPF), also known as the Worker's Investment and Savings Program (WISP), is a compulsory savings plan that supplements the benefits from the regular SSS program. It's a key part of the Social Security Act of 2018, designed to provide a more substantial retirement fund for members.

What is it?

WISP is a safe, convenient, and tax-free retirement savings plan. Contributions are automatically deducted from a member's salary and are invested by the SSS to generate earnings, which are credited annually to the member's account.

Who is Covered?

It is mandatory for all private-sector employees, self-employed individuals, and OFWs whose Monthly Salary Credit (MSC) exceeds ₱20,000. Contributions to WISP are made on the portion of the MSC above this threshold.

Key Benefits

  • Higher retirement benefits.
  • Tax-free earnings.
  • Professionally managed funds.
  • Guaranteed earnings.

Pag-IBIG Fund Benefits

The Home Development Mutual Fund (HDMF), more popularly known as the Pag-IBIG Fund, is a national savings program and an affordable home financing institution for Filipino workers. Membership is mandatory for all SSS and GSIS-covered employees.

Provident Savings Program

A mandatory savings program where members contribute a monthly amount, which is matched by their employer. These savings earn annual dividends. Members can also opt for the MP2 Savings, a voluntary program with higher dividend rates.

Key Features:

  • Government-guaranteed savings.
  • Tax-free dividends.
  • Withdrawal upon retirement, disability, or 240 months of contributions.

Affordable Housing Loan

The flagship program of the fund, allowing members to borrow up to P6 million to purchase or construct a home, with low interest rates and long repayment terms.

Eligibility Summary:

  • At least 24 monthly savings.
  • Not more than 65 years old at the time of loan application.
  • Has the legal capacity to acquire and encumber real property.

Multi-Purpose Loan (MPL)

A cash loan designed to help members with their short-term financial needs, such as medical expenses, education, or minor home improvements.

Eligibility Summary:

  • At least 24 monthly savings.
  • Sufficient proof of income.

Calamity Loan

Provides immediate financial aid to members residing in areas declared under a state of calamity by the Office of the President or the local Sanggunian.

Eligibility Summary:

  • At least 24 monthly savings.
  • Resides in an area declared under a state of calamity.
  • Must apply within 90 days from the declaration of calamity.

Pag-IBIG Loyalty Card Plus

Acts as a primary identification card for members and provides access to exclusive discounts and rewards from hundreds of partner establishments nationwide. It also serves as a cash card where members can receive loan proceeds and MP2 Savings dividends.

Key Features:

  • Serves as an official ID card for Pag-IBIG transactions.
  • Functions as an ATM/debit card for easy fund access.
  • Offers discounts on groceries, fuel, tuition fees, hospital bills, and more.

2025 Contribution Rates & Examples

For employed members, contributions are shared between the employee and the employer, based on the member's Monthly Fund Salary (MFS), which is capped at ₱10,000.

₱1,500 and below 1% 2%
Over ₱1,500 2% 2%

Example 1: Salary of ₱8,000

  • Employee Share: ₱8,000 × 2% = ₱160.00
  • Employer Share: ₱8,000 × 2% = ₱160.00

  • Employee Share: ₱10,000 × 2% = ₱200.00 (Max)
  • Employer Share: ₱10,000 × 2% = ₱200.00 (Max)
  • Total Monthly Contribution: ₱400.00

PhilHealth Benefits Overview

The Philippine Health Insurance Corporation (PhilHealth) is the national health insurance program, tasked to provide universal health coverage for all Filipinos. Membership is mandatory and aims to provide affordable and accessible healthcare services.

Inpatient Benefits

Covers expenses for room and board, drugs and medicines, laboratory exams, and operating room fees when a member is confined in an accredited hospital for at least 24 hours.

Programs Include:

  • All Case Rates (fixed amount for specific medical cases).
  • Coverage for professional fees of attending physicians.

Outpatient Benefits

Covers day surgeries, radiotherapy, hemodialysis, and outpatient blood transfusion. This allows members to receive medical care without the need for hospital confinement.

Programs Include:

  • Hemodialysis and Peritoneal Dialysis Sessions.
  • Chemotherapy and Radiotherapy sessions.

Z Benefits Package

Designed to help with financially catastrophic illnesses. It provides substantial financial support for treatments that are often costly.

Covers treatments for:

  • Leukemia, Breast Cancer, Prostate Cancer.
  • Kidney Transplant, Coronary Artery Bypass Graft.

Maternity Care Package (MCP)

Covers prenatal care, normal spontaneous delivery services, and postpartum care in accredited hospitals, birthing homes, or maternity clinics.

Programs Include:

  • Antenatal care (at least 4 visits).
  • Normal delivery and postpartum care services.

Konsulta Package

A primary care benefit package that includes initial and follow-up consultations, health screening, and access to selected diagnostic services and medicines.

Services Include:

  • Consultation and case management.
  • Labs: CBC, Urinalysis, Chest X-ray.

SDG-Related Benefits

Packages aimed at achieving Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), focusing on major public health concerns.

Programs Include:

  • Outpatient Anti-Tuberculosis Treatment (DOTS).
  • HIV-AIDS Treatment Package.
  • Malaria Package.

A Note for HR Practitioners

Successfully administering these statutory benefits is more than a legal requirement; it's a cornerstone of building a positive and supportive workplace. How these benefits are communicated and managed directly impacts employee morale, engagement, and loyalty. Here are key strategies for HR managers to ensure benefits are not just provided, but also understood and valued by every employee.

Proactive Onboarding

Clearly explain all statutory benefits during the new hire orientation. Provide a detailed employee handbook (digital or physical) that they can reference anytime.

Regular Communication

Send out regular reminders and updates on new regulations (like annual SSS table changes) through email, company portals, or bulletin boards.

Provide Accessible Support

Designate a go-to person or an HR channel for all benefit-related questions. Ensure employees feel comfortable asking for help with SSS, Pag-IBIG, or PhilHealth applications.

Streamline Processes

Establish clear, efficient procedures for enrollment, claims, and other benefit-related transactions. Timely processing is critical to building trust.

By treating benefits administration as a key part of the employee experience, HR can transform it from a compliance task into a powerful tool for retention.
